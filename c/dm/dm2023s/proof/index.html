1.7.5
Direct Proof
Direct proof of a conditional statement \( p \rightarrow q\).
Assume \(p\) is T.
Use rules of inference, with the final step showing \(q\) must be T.
Ex 1:
Give a direct proof of the theorem "If n is an odd number, then n2 is odd."

Proof by Contraposition 反證法
Conditional statement \( p \rightarrow q\) can be proved by its contrapositive \( \neg q \rightarrow \neg p\) is T.
Assume \( \neg q\), use axioms, definitions, theorems, rules of inference, we show that \( \neg p \) must follow.

VACUOUS PROOFS 空泛證明
If we can show that p is false, then we have a proof, called a vacuous proof, of the conditional statement p → q.
Ex 5:
Show that the proposition P(0) is true, where P(n) is “If n > 1, then n2 > n” and the domain consists of all integers.
TRIVIAL PROOFS 平庸證明
A proof of p → q that uses the fact that q is true is called a trivial proof. Trivial proofs are often important in mathematical induction.
Ex 7:
Let P(n) be “If a and b are positive integers with a ≥ b, then an ≥ bn,” where the domain consists of all nonnegative integers. Show that P(0) is true.
1.7.7
Proofs by Contradiction 歸謬證法
Suppose we want to prove that a statement \(p\) is true. We can prove that \(p\) is true if we can show that \(¬p → (r ∧ ¬r)\) is true for some proposition \(r\). Proofs of this type are called proofs by contradiction.
Ex 11:
Prove that \(√2\) is irrational by giving a proof by contradiction.
1.7.7
Proofs by Contradiction 歸謬證法
Proof by contradiction can be used to prove conditional statements. In such proofs, we first assume the negation of the conclusion.We then use the premises of the theorem and the negation of the conclusion to arrive at a contradiction. (The reason that such proofs are valid rests on the logical equivalence of \(p → q\) and \((p ∧ ¬q) → F\).
Note that we can rewrite a proof by contraposition of a conditional statement as a proof by contradiction. In a proof of \(p → q\) by contraposition, we assume that \(¬q\) is true. We then show that \(¬p\) must also be true. To rewrite a proof by contraposition of \(p → q\) as a proof by contradiction, we suppose that both \(p\) and \(¬q\) are true. Then, we use the steps from the proof of \(¬q → ¬p\) to show that \(¬p\) is true. This leads to the contradiction \(p ∧ ¬p\), completing the proof.
Example 11 illustrates how a proof by contraposition of a conditional statement can be rewritten as a proof by contradiction.
Ex 12:
Give a proof by contradiction of the theorem “If 3n + 2 is odd, then n is odd.”
PROOFS OF EQUIVALENCE
To prove a theorem that is a biconditional statement, that is, a statement of the form p ↔ q, we show that p → q and q → p are both true.
Ex 13:
Prove the theorem “If n is an integer, then n is odd if and only if n2 is odd.”
